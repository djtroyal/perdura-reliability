Perdura Mathematical & Statistical Methodology Audit

A traceable audit of Perdura's reliability, statistical, physics-of-failure, prediction, quality, and decision algorithms, with reproducible probes and a prioritized remediation backlog.

Jul 10, 2026, 6:00 PM UTC

Technical summary

Perdura has a broad, well-tested calculation surface, but it is not yet uniformly decision-grade. The audit identified five correctness blockers that can silently change a reliability, life, or maintenance decision: system mission-rate units, the RDT allowable-failure solver, an FTA method mislabeled as exact, step-stress cumulative exposure, and pseudo interval-censoring in degradation analysis.

The register contains 50 findings: 5 Critical, 21 High, 21 Medium, and 3 Low. Thirty-five are High-confidence findings grounded in direct code traces and/or independent numerical checks. The affected P0 outputs should be gated until corrected; unaffected modules should be described by their own evidence and assumptions rather than receiving a blanket pass or fail.

The baseline of 946 passing tests is valuable regression evidence, not a validation certificate. At least two tests preserve incorrect units or reversed semantics by recomputing the implementation's own convention. The remediation program therefore starts with analytic identities, published worked examples, adversarial boundaries, simulation coverage, and explicit result-quality states.

Key findings

  1. Dimensional correctness is not enforced end-to-end. Per-part mission calculations convert FPMH correctly, but the system aggregation omits the same conversion; PoF endpoints also need consistent unit/range contracts.
  2. Several algorithms return a plausible boundary or approximation under a stronger label. Examples include zero allowable RDT failures, a grid maximum called an optimum, and a cut-set bound stored under exact.
  3. Inference is often more fragile than the UI implies. Fitted-parameter KS tests use unadjusted critical values; AICc becomes ordinary AIC when undefined; optimizer termination and identifiability are inconsistently enforced; Wald/delta intervals dominate difficult small/censored problems.
  4. Named methods sometimes exceed their implementation. PAND is ordinary AND, and multiple HRA and standards-branded prediction functions are simplified screening calculations. Method identity and conformance tier must become explicit output metadata.
  5. Workflow preconditions are missing in quality and predictive analysis. Capability can be reported without stability; SPC lacks a clean Phase-I/Phase-II boundary; Gage R&R does not reliably distinguish balanced, unbalanced, crossed, or nested designs; predictive splits and encoders do not always respect data structure.

Scope, data, and definitions

The review covered all 32 public core modules and all 21 API calculation domains: 295 public core classes/functions, 114 endpoints, and 28,607 Python lines. Tier 1 domains received static formula tracing plus at least one direct numerical, analytic, or standards-comparison probe. Tier 2 domains received static formula, input-validation, assumption, and test-coverage review with targeted public-source research. Tier is audit depth—not a quality rating.

Severity means: Critical can silently reverse or materially change a primary engineering decision; High can materially bias a result or overstate validity under realistic use; Medium is a consequential assumption, semantic, workflow, or model-adequacy gap; Low is a guardrail or portfolio improvement with limited immediate decision impact. Confidence is High when directly reproduced or unambiguous in code and Medium when the issue is chiefly model adequacy, conformance scope, or a method choice requiring domain calibration.

Methodology

The audit used five linked passes. First, an AST-backed inventory reconciled public core symbols and decorated endpoints. Second, formulas, units, censoring semantics, optimizer control, assumptions, and UI labels were traced from request to response. Third, high-risk paths were challenged with analytic special cases and adversarial numerical probes. Fourth, tests were reviewed for what they actually establish—regression consistency, reference parity, or neither. Fifth, alternatives were researched using public primary publications and official NIST, NRC, NASA, ECSS, FIDES, SciPy, and scikit-learn documentation.

A finding was registered only when it had a concrete code trace, reproducible behavior, explicit assumption gap, or defensible method/conformance mismatch. Recommendations preserve simple fast methods where they are useful, but require an output-quality label such as validated, approximate, screening, insufficient-data, or non-converged. No production calculation code was changed during this audit.

Model validation assessment

Current coverage is strong enough to catch many software regressions, but uneven for mathematical validity. A robust validation architecture should have four layers: (1) analytic identities and dimensional invariance; (2) public worked examples with edition/clause provenance; (3) seeded simulation recovery and empirical coverage for estimators, intervals, and tests; and (4) metamorphic tests such as group-order sign reversal, equivalent network representations, time-unit invariance, and monotonicity.

For fitted lifetime and ALT models, every result should expose convergence, gradient/objective finiteness, boundary contact, design rank, identifiability, use-stress extrapolation, and uncertainty method. For standards-based prediction, the required model card is edition + clause/table + scope + validation examples + conformance tier. For process capability and control, stability and baseline phase are prerequisites, not optional notes. For system models, independence/common-cause assumptions and exact/bound/simulation status must be part of the result schema.

The highest-value alternate methods are: log-domain likelihoods; profile likelihood and parametric bootstrap; refitted-bootstrap GoF tests; BDD/ZBDD fault-tree evaluation with common-cause extensions; Nelson recurrent-event MCF and cluster bootstrap; REML mixed models for MSA; Phase-I/Phase-II SPC with exact or Laney attribute charts; grouped/time-aware predictive validation and calibration; virtual-age/semi-Markov maintenance; and uncertainty/sensitivity families for PoF.

Recommended next steps

Complete the five P0 items before treating the affected outputs as decision-grade. Then deliver P1 as a shared numerical-integrity release rather than isolated patches: log-domain probabilities, fit eligibility, optimizer diagnostics, calibrated uncertainty/GoF, correct importance/allocation semantics, and regression/predictive guardrails. P2 adds model adequacy and workflow depth; P3 expands the portfolio.

Compatibility should be explicit. Corrected units and reversed confidence-band names warrant versioned responses or temporary aliases with warnings. Approximate methods may remain, but their names and status must say what they are. Every backlog item has a finding mapping and a testable definition of done in the supporting implementation backlog.

Limitations, uncertainty, and robustness

This is a source-and-algorithm audit with selected adversarial probes, not an independent regulatory certification or exhaustive proof over every input combination. No proprietary standards text or private validation dataset was available, so standards findings concern traceability, public-scope comparison, and claims discipline rather than clause-complete certification. Tier 2 review is intentionally shallower than Tier 1. Model-adequacy recommendations require calibration to Perdura's intended industries, loss functions, and data regimes.

The exact numerical probes are high-confidence within insignificant floating-point variation. Findings about coverage, uncertainty, dependency, model family, or conformance are sometimes Medium-confidence because the appropriate replacement depends on intended use. A clean result in one domain does not certify another, and absence of a material finding in a sampled path does not prove absence of defects.

Further questions

  • Which outputs are used for contractual, regulatory, safety, or capital-allocation decisions? That determines the validation burden and release gates.
  • Can corrected unit/semantic responses introduce a versioned API, or must compatibility be preserved through aliases and warnings?
  • Which authoritative worked examples and licensed standards editions can be supplied for conformance testing?
  • Are field datasets available for calibration drift, predictive validation, process stability, common-cause failures, repairable-system heterogeneity, and PoF uncertainty?
  • Who will serve as qualified reviewers for HRA, standards prediction, PoF, and process metrology before those methods receive a conforming or validated badge?
